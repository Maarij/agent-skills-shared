# Blue Pencil

A blue pencil is the editor's tool for cutting. This skill cuts the generic out of product writing, so what's left reads as written by a specific person who knows the product, not assembled from the statistical average of everything ever written.

## When this applies

Use it for **user-facing copy** (UI strings, errors, tooltips, marketing, metadata, aria-labels) and **user-facing or product docs** (README intros, landing pages, help and onboarding text). Not internal engineering docs, code comments, or commit messages.

**Defer to the project's own voice first.** If the project has a voice rubric (a `copy-voice.md`, a style guide, a `CONTEXT.md` glossary, brand docs), that is authoritative for register, tone, terminology, and any legal framing. This skill adds a cross-project anti-slop layer on top. It never overrides the local register, and it never flags a term the local rubric explicitly sanctions.

## The one principle

**Don't substitute form for content. Be specific, name the thing, show the consequence.**

The test: *could this exact line appear, unchanged, in any other app's copy?* If yes, it isn't doing work. "Unlock powerful insights" could sell anything, so it sells nothing. "See which accounts you're leaving untouched" could only belong to one product. Write the second kind.

## The accumulation principle

The real AI tell is **density**, not any single word. One "robust" is nothing. Three "robust"s, a "seamless," and a "from setup to scale" in one paragraph is the tell. Don't witch-hunt a lone em dash or a single "and"-opener. Watch for the pattern of reaching for the most probable word every time. Variety is the signal of a person writing; sameness is the signal of a model completing.

## The positive baseline

What good product copy converges on, whatever the project:

- Plain words over Latinate ("use," not "utilize"; "help," not "facilitate").
- Varied sentence rhythm. Uniform cadence is the single strongest AI tell.
- Contractions as the default register ("you'll," "there's").
- Concrete over abstract. Name the actual thing, number, or action.
- Warnings state what happened plus one concrete next step.
- No self-praise adjectives about the product ("simple," "powerful," "seamless"). Show it, don't claim it.

## Three modes

**1. Write-time.** Before drafting, skim [BANS.md](BANS.md). Write to the positive baseline. When you reach for a word on the ban list, stop and name what's actually happening instead.

**2. Self-review (always, before you finish).** Re-read the draft once, looking for density rather than perfection. Run the quick-scan in [BANS.md](BANS.md) against it. For each hit, ask: first appearance (often fine) or third (a crutch)? Rewrite the crutches. Then confirm you kept the local rubric's register and terms.

**3. Sweep (on request).** When asked to audit a project's existing copy for AI tells, follow [SWEEP.md](SWEEP.md).

## What to do instead

Every ban has the same fix, listed next to it in [BANS.md](BANS.md): describe the specific thing, state the concrete consequence, or cut. If a line exists only to sound polished, cut it. If a claim is generic, make it specific or delete it.
